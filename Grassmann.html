Introduction

A general many body state of Bosons can be written in the following form \begin{equation} |\Psi \rangle = \sum_{n_1,n_2,...} c_{n_1,n_2,...} |n_1,n_2,... \rangle \end{equation} where \( c_{n_1,n_2,...} \) are complex expansion coefficients. To the end of introducing the path integral, it is desirable to device a minimal uncertainty state, since the Feynman path integral is a concept which evolves around a classical action. (to be defined) Such a minimal uncertainty state is called a coherent state, and is defined as follows \begin{equation} |\alpha\rangle = e^{\sum_{j} \alpha_j \hat{a}^{\dagger}_j} |0\rangle \end{equation} where \(\alpha_j \in \mathrm{C}\). By way of the identity, \([\hat{a},(\hat{a}^{\dagger})^n]=n(\hat{a}^{\dagger})^{n-1} \) \begin{equation} \hat{a}|\alpha\rangle = \alpha |\alpha \rangle \end{equation} Next, we may attempt to develop a fermionic coherent state. Proceeding along the lines above will lead us into trouble. Instead of deriving the following theory rigorously, we will develop the theory from the simplest example. For a system with one fermionic species, define an ansatz coherent state \begin{equation} |\theta \rangle = e^{\rho \theta \hat{c}^{\dagger}} |0\rangle \end{equation} whence we get \begin{equation} \hat{c} |\theta \rangle = \rho\hat{c} \theta \hat{c}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \end{equation} \begin{equation} \theta |\theta \rangle = \theta |0\rangle + \rho \theta \theta \hat{c}^{\dagger} |0\rangle \end{equation} \begin{equation} \hat{c}|\theta\rangle = \theta |\theta \rangle \end{equation} is satisfied by \(\theta^2=0 \), \( \hat{c}\theta=-\theta\hat{c}\) and \(\rho=-1\). In fact, the coherent state encompassing all fermionic species of the problem \begin{equation} |\theta \rangle =e^{-\sum_{j}\theta_j \hat{c}_j} |0\rangle \end{equation} must obey similar properties in order for it to be a coherent state. Namely, \begin{equation}\label{xx} \begin{split} &\theta_i \theta_j + \theta_j \theta_i=0 \\ &\hat{c}_i \theta_j + \theta_j \hat{c}_i=0 \\ \end{split} \end{equation} Grassmann numbers commute with complex numbers since they are defined as a vector space over the complex numbers. Further, the coherent states can be used to reformulate expectation values in terms of path integrals. \rangle \end{equation} For one fermionic species, the integral for the partition function becomes \begin{equation} \mathcal{Z} = \int d \overline{\psi} d \psi e^{-\overline{\psi} \psi} \langle -\psi Now, the most important take away from the introduction to path integrals is how we proceed in calculating correlation functions. By following the steps leading to the path integral formulation of the partition function, we obtain \begin{equation} \langle \hat{\tau} \hat{c}(\tau)\hat{c}^{\dagger}(0)\rangle = \frac{\int D[\overline{\psi},\psi] {\psi}(\tau) \overline{\psi(0)} e^{-S[\overline{\psi},\psi]} } {\int D[\overline{\psi},\psi] e^{-S[\overline{\psi},\psi]}} \end{equation} Another concept which will provide useful is to integrate out fermion species in the theory. Suppose we have a partition function of the following form \begin{equation} \int d\overline{\phi}d\phi d\overline{\psi}d\psi e^{-(A \overline{\phi}\phi + B(\overline{\psi}\phi + \overline{\phi}\psi + D \overline{\phi}\phi ) )} = \int d\overline{\phi}d\phi e^{ - (D-\frac{B^2}{A})} \overline{\phi} \phi \end{equation} where A,B and C are complex numbers. This identity is demonstrated by performing a Taylor expansion of the left hand side and evaluate the action of the integral operators on various terms.
