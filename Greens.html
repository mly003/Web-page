Introduction

Time evolution pictures

Heisenberg picture: All dynamics is contained in the operators, i.e., \begin{equation} i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\hat{O} =i[\hat{H},\hat{O}] \end{equation} Every expectation value is formed from a time dependent operator on a time-independent state or an ensemble of such states. All expectation values are taken with respect to the grand canonical ensemble \begin{equation} \hat{\rho}=\frac{e^{-\beta\hat{\mathcal{H}}} }{Z} \end{equation} were \( Z=tr \{e^{-\beta\hat{\mathcal{H}}}\} \) and we tacitly denote \(\hat{\mathcal{H}}= \hat{ \mathcal{H} }_{0} -\mu \hat{N} \). In this way, \begin{equation} \langle\hat{O}\rangle=\sum_{n} \langle n|\hat{\rho}\hat{O}|n\rangle\ \end{equation} Greens functions \begin{equation} G_{\alpha,\beta}(t,t')=-i\langle \hat{T}\hat{c}_{\alpha}(t)\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{\beta}(t')\rangle \end{equation} where \( \hat{T}\) is the time ordering operator and \(\alpha,\beta\) denote quantum numbers (e.g. spin, orbital, momentum etc.) In the context of nonequilibrium systems - we must be more careful, and state the expectation value as follows, \begin{equation} G_{\alpha,\beta}(t,t')= -i\langle \hat{T}\hat{U}(0,t)\hat{c}_{\alpha}(t)\hat{U}(t,t')\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{\beta}(t')\hat{U}(t',0)\rangle \end{equation} which in the time-independent case evaluates to the simpler expression above. We will touch upon various variants of this expression later, but right now we will focus on the retarded Green's function, defined as follows \begin{equation} G^{R}_{\alpha,\beta}(t,t')=-i \theta(t-t')\langle\{\hat{c}_{\alpha}(t),\hat{c}_{\beta}^{\dagger}(t')\}\rangle \end{equation} where \(\theta(t)\) is the heaviside step function. Upon differentiating \(G^{R}\) with respect to t, we obtain \begin{equation} i\frac{\partial}{\partial t}G^R_{\alpha,\beta} =\delta(t-t') \langle\{\hat{c}_{\alpha}(t),\hat{c}_{\beta}^{\dagger}(t')\}\rangle +\theta(t-t')\langle\{i[\hat{\mathcal{H}},\hat{c}_{\alpha}(t)],\hat{c}^{\dagger}_{\beta}(t')\}\rangle \end{equation} In order to proceed with an explicit expression, we need to specify a system Hamiltonian. To that end, we simply define the Hamiltonian of a non-interacting system of electrons (in a periodic potential) \begin{equation} \hat{\mathcal{H}}=\sum_{\boldsymbol{k}} \epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})\hat{c}_{\boldsymbol{k}}^{\dagger}\hat{c}_{\boldsymbol{k}} \end{equation} Now, The Green's \begin{equation} i\frac{\partial}{\partial t}G^R_{\boldsymbol{k}} =\delta(t-t')\delta_{\sigma,\sigma'} -i\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})\theta(t-t')\langle\{\hat{c}_{\boldsymbol{k}}(t),\hat{c}_{\boldsymbol{k}}^{\dagger}(t')\}\rangle \end{equation} or alternatively \begin{equation} [i\frac{\partial}{\partial t} -\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k}) ]G^R_{\boldsymbol{k}} =\delta(t-t')\delta_{\sigma,\sigma'} \end{equation} Which evidently is solved by \begin{equation} G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(t,t')=-i\theta(t-t')e^{-i\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})(t-t')} \end{equation} Owing to the fact that \( G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(t,t')= G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(t-t')\) it is readily verified that the Fourier transformed expression is given by \begin{equation} G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})+i\eta^{+}} \end{equation} where \(\eta^{+}\) is a positive infinitesimal. To actually describe something about our system, we would like to introduce the concept of a spectral function \begin{equation} A_{\boldsymbol{k}}(\omega)=-\frac{1}{\pi}Im{G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(\omega)} \end{equation} By the Dirac identity \(\frac{1}{x+i\eta^{+}}=\mathcal{P}(\frac{1}{x})-i\pi\delta(x)\), the spectral function of our system is \begin{equation} A_{\boldsymbol{k}}(\omega)=\delta(\omega-\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})) \end{equation} The quantity \(A(\omega)d\omega\) gives the probability to find and electron in the infinitesimal energy window \([\omega,\omega+d\omega]\) Green's functions for interacting systems are characterized by a self energy appearing in the spectral function. \begin{equation} G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(\omega)=\frac{1}{\omega-\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})+\Sigma+i\eta^{+}} \label{eq:full} \end{equation} \( \Sigma\) can be comprised of a real and imaginary part. Now, the real part will only give rise to a shift in the position of the singularity, whereas the imaginary part will give rise to a broadening of the spectral function. The latter property is best illustrated by re-writing the expression [\ref{eq:full}] as \begin{equation} A_{\boldsymbol{k}}(\omega) = \frac{(\eta^{+}+Im{\Sigma})^2}{(\omega+Re(\Sigma)-\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k}))^2+(\eta^{+} + Im(\Sigma))^2} \end{equation} The above expression can be seen to arise from a damped Green's function in the time domain \begin{equation} G_{\boldsymbol{k}}^R(t,t')=-i\theta(t-t')e^{-i\epsilon(\boldsymbol{k})(t-t')-Im(\Sigma)(t-t'} \end{equation} As a result, the single particle states can be said to have a finite lifetime. Another thing that is connected to the spectral function is the notion of a sum rule. We will first define the sum rule for an equilibrium spectral function. In the Lehmann representation, the spectral function can be cast as \begin{equation} A_{\boldsymbol{k}}(\omega)=\frac{1}{\mathcal{Z}}\sum_{n,m}|\langle n|\hat{c}_{\boldsymbol{k}}| m\rangle|^2 (e^{-\beta E_n} + e^{-\beta E_m}) \delta(\omega + E_n-E_m) \end{equation} For which the sum rule is the following identity, \begin{equation} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d\omega A_{\boldsymbol{k}}(\omega)=1 \end{equation} The sum rule is a useful benchmark. We will later see the a corresponding non equilibrium analogue of a sum rule.

Imaginary time Green's functions - Matsubara Green's functions

Let time be imaginary, and define a real quantity \(\tau=it\). An imaginary time Green's function can be defined as follows \begin{equation} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(\tau) = -i\langle \hat{T}_{\tau} \hat{c}_{\alpha}(\tau) \hat{c}_{\beta}^{\dagger}(0)\rangle \end{equation} where we let \(-\beta \lt \tau \lt \beta \) . The evolution the operators in imaginary time is \begin{equation} \hat{O}(\tau)=e^{\hat{H}\tau}\hat{O}e^{-\hat{H}\tau} \end{equation} Furthermore, the Matsubara Green's functions are anti-periodic in \(\tau\) due to the cyclicity of the trace. For \(\tau\lt 0\) we have \begin{equation} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(\tau) = \epsilon \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(\tau+\beta) \end{equation} with \(\epsilon=-1\) (\(\epsilon=-1\)) for fermions (bosons). Due to the \(2\beta\) periodicity of the Green's function, we can define a fourier transformation as follows \begin{equation} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(i\omega_n) =\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\beta}^{\beta} d\tau e^{i\omega_n} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(\tau) \end{equation} where \(\omega_n =\frac{2\pi n}{2\beta} \). By way of a variable change, and using the anti-periodicity, it is readily shown that \begin{equation} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(i\omega_n) = \int_{0}^{\beta} d\tau e^{i\omega_n} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(\tau) \end{equation} where n is an even integer for bosons and an odd integer for fermions. The correspondence between the imaginary time Green's function and the retarded Green's function is realized through analytical continuation. To this end, define a Lehmann representation of the imaginary time G-function, and assume \(\tau \gt 0\) \begin{equation} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(\tau) = -\frac{i}{\mathcal{Z}}\sum_{n,m} e^{-\beta E_n + E_n \tau - E_m\tau} \langle n| \hat{c}_{\alpha}|m\rangle\langle m|\hat{c}_{\beta}^{\dagger}|n\rangle \end{equation} Performing the transformation to frequency space, we get \begin{equation} \mathcal{G}_{\alpha,\beta}(i\omega_k) =-\frac{i}{\mathcal{Z}} \sum_{n,m} e^{-\beta E_n} \langle n| \hat{c}_{\alpha}|m\rangle\langle m|\hat{c}_{\beta}^{\dagger}|n\rangle \frac{( {e^{i\omega_k \beta +\beta E_n \tau - E_m\tau}} -1)}{i\omega_k + E_n -E_m} \\ \end{equation}
